Prof. Fekri’s research group has a multidisciplinary flavor, working in overlapping areas of coding theory, information theory, communication security, and signal processing. Prof. Fekri and his research group are investigating the fundamental limits and obtaining optimal protocols for systems, networks, and mathematical models/objects resulting from the overall conduct of large collections of basic units that are confined to interact with their neighboring units. In particular, the research has explored theory and practice of low-density parity-check codes (LDPC) oriented toward solving coding problems in wireless communications, optical networks, and data storage. The team has published several papers in LDPC for parallel channels, rate-compatible coding, unequal error protection, improved iterative decoding algorithms, and LDPC codes with the linear minimum distance property.

Additionally, the research has investigated the application of mathematical tools to modern networking, spanning from theoretical characterization of wired/wireless networks to the design, analysis, and optimization of communication protocols. The team has published several papers in the study of throughput for erasure networks with finite buffers, performance characterization of sensor networks and sparse intermittently connected mobile environments, asymptotic analysis of connectivity and coverage properties of sensor networks, and design and analysis of reliable content dissemination schemes in such networks using fountain codes.

Further, employing design concepts and ideologies from random codes, the group has developed novel methods for securing wireless networks.
This includes random key pre-distributions schemes, secure broadcasting algorithms, and iterative trust and reputation management schemes. Finally, Prof. Fekri and his team have advanced the study of wavelets and filter banks over Galois fields. The research introduced the application of this theory to various error-correcting codes. The team also developed the first
cryptographic system using finite field wavelet transforms.
